# Billy Wilder's 10 Rules of Screenwriting

## Summary
Ten rules distilled from *Conversations with Wilder* (1999) by Cameron Crowe, drawn from interviews with Wilder conducted between 1996 and 1999. Wilder was 90 years old at the time. The rules are terse, aphoristic, and structural — less about inspiration than about discipline and audience awareness.

## The 10 Rules

1. The audience is fickle.
2. Grab 'em by the throat and never let 'em go.
3. Develop a clean line of action for your leading character.
4. Know where you're going.
5. The more subtle and elegant you are in hiding your plot points, the better you are as a writer.
6. If you have a problem with the third act, the real problem is in the first act.
7. A tip from Lubitsch: Let the audience add up two plus two. They'll love you forever.
8. In doing voice-overs, be careful not to describe what the audience already sees. Add to what they're seeing.
9. The event that occurs at the second act curtain triggers the end of the movie.
10. The third act must build, build, build in tempo and action until the last event, and then — that's it. Don't hang around.

## Key Takeaways

**Rules 1-2 — Audience first:** The audience's attention is never guaranteed. Every scene must earn it. This connects directly to [[concepts/stakes]] — if nothing is at risk, attention drifts.

**Rule 3 — Protagonist clarity:** A leading character must have a visible, active goal at all times. Connects to [[concepts/protagonist]] (page not yet created). The audience follows action, not mood.

**Rule 4 — Know your destination:** You cannot hide your [[concepts/turning-point]] elegantly if you don't know where the story is going. Structure must be planned, not discovered in draft.

**Rule 5 — Hide the machinery:** Plot points should feel inevitable in retrospect, not telegraphed. The craft is in concealment. Connects to [[concepts/subtext]].

**Rule 6 — Third act problems are first act problems:** If the climax doesn't land, the setup failed. [[concepts/stakes]], [[concepts/inciting-incident]], and character establishment in Act 1 determine whether Act 3 earns its emotion.

**Rule 7 — The Lubitsch Touch:** Trust the audience's intelligence. Implying is stronger than stating. The audience's active participation in completing meaning creates emotional investment. Connects to [[concepts/subtext]].

**Rule 8 — Voice-over discipline:** Narration must add information, never repeat visuals. Economy of means — every element earns its place.

**Rule 9 — Act 2 curtain triggers the end:** The [[concepts/turning-point]] at the end of Act 2 is not just a transition — it determines the shape of the entire [[concepts/climax]]. Plant it carefully.

**Rule 10 — Third act velocity:** [[concepts/resolution]] must be brief. Once the [[concepts/climax]] fires, stop. Momentum is everything. Don't hang around.
