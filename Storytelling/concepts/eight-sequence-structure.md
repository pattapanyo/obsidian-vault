# Eight-Sequence Structure

## Definition
A granular structural framework that divides a screenplay into eight sequences of roughly 10-15 minutes each, every sequence functioning as a mini-movie with its own tension arc. Developed by [[entities/Frank Daniel]] at USC, popularized by [[entities/Paul Gulino]].

## How It Works

| Sequence | Function | Act |
|----------|----------|-----|
| 1 | Status quo established, inciting incident arrives | Act 1 |
| 2 | Protagonist faces predicament, gets locked into the story | Act 1 → 2 |
| 3 | First attempts to solve the problem, false hope | Act 2A |
| 4 | Midpoint — situation intensifies, stakes clarify | Act 2A → 2B |
| 5 | Complications escalate, options narrow | Act 2B |
| 6 | Major setback — darkest moment, all seems lost | Act 2B |
| 7 | Climax attempt — protagonist makes final move | Act 2 → 3 |
| 8 | Resolution — consequences, new equilibrium | Act 3 |

## Why It Matters
Eight-sequence structure solves the core problem of [[concepts/three-act-structure]]: it tells you *what to write next*, especially inside the long Act 2. Each sequence has a clear goal and outcome, giving the writer a series of manageable targets rather than one vast middle to fill.

## Relationship to Three-Act Structure
These are not competing systems — they are different zoom levels of the same story. See [[synthesis/three-act-vs-eight-sequence]] for a full comparison.

## Examples
- **Raiders of the Lost Ark (1981):** Often cited as a textbook eight-sequence film. Sequence 1 ends with Indy getting the mission. Sequence 4 midpoint is Marion's apparent death. Sequence 6 darkest moment is Indy chained to the stake at the ritual.
