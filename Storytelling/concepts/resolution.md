# Resolution

## Definition
The final movement of a screenplay after the [[concepts/climax]], showing the consequences of the confrontation and the new equilibrium the story's world has reached.

## How It Works
Resolution is Sequence 8 of [[concepts/eight-sequence-structure]]. It answers the question: *what has changed?*

A resolution does not need to be happy — it needs to be honest to the story's logic. It shows:
- The protagonist's new state (changed or tragically unchanged)
- The world's new state
- The thematic payoff — what the story was really saying

## The Character Arc Payoff
Resolution is where [[concepts/character-arc]] lands. If the protagonist grew through the story, the resolution shows who they have become. If they refused to change (tragedy), the resolution shows the cost of that refusal.

## Common Mistakes
- **Too long** — resolution should be brief. Once the climax fires, audiences disengage quickly
- **Too neat** — false resolution that erases real cost feels dishonest
- **Confused with climax** — resolution shows aftermath, it does not contain the final confrontation

## Examples
- **The Godfather (1972):** Michael sitting at the desk as the door closes on Kay — the resolution is a single image showing his transformation is complete and irreversible
- **Chinatown (1974):** "Forget it Jake, it's Chinatown" — resolution that confirms nothing has changed and nothing will
- **Alien (1979):** Ripley's log entry in the escape pod — calm after horror, survival confirmed
