# Stakes

## Definition
What the protagonist stands to lose if they fail. Stakes are the emotional engine that makes the audience care about the outcome of every scene, sequence, and act.

## How It Works
Stakes operate on three levels simultaneously in the best screenplays:

| Level | Description | Example (Alien) |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Physical** | Life, safety, survival | Ripley will die |
| **Professional** | Status, mission, purpose | The crew's duty to the company |
| **Emotional/Psychological** | Identity, relationships, belief | Ripley's sense of self as a survivor |

Weak scripts often have only physical stakes. Strong scripts stack all three — so even when physical danger is absent, something meaningful is still at risk.

## Stakes Must Escalate
Stakes are not static. They should increase at every [[concepts/turning-point]] and reach maximum pressure at the [[concepts/climax]]. If stakes feel the same in Act 2 as they did in Act 1, the story is stalling.

## The Audience Proxy
Stakes only work if the audience believes in them. This is why character work in Act 1 is essential — we need to understand what matters to the protagonist before we can fear losing it alongside them.

## Examples
- **Chinatown (1974):** Stakes escalate from professional (solve the case) to personal (protect Evelyn) to civilizational (the water supply of Los Angeles)
- **The Godfather (1972):** Stakes shift from family loyalty to personal morality to Michael's soul
- **Alien (1979):** Physical stakes are constant but emotional stakes — who survives, who is expendable — create the real tension
