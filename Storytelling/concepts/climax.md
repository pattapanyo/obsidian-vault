# Climax

## Definition
The moment of maximum dramatic tension in a screenplay where the central conflict is finally confronted and resolved. It is the inevitable collision between the protagonist's will and the story's opposing force.

## How It Works
The climax is the payoff of everything the story has built. It arrives in Sequence 7 of [[concepts/eight-sequence-structure]] and is distinct from the resolution that follows it.

Three qualities of a strong climax:
- **Inevitable** — feels like the only possible destination given everything before it
- **Personal** — the protagonist must make an active choice, not be rescued by circumstance
- **Costly** — something real is lost or sacrificed, even in victory

## Climax vs Resolution
These are often confused but are separate beats:

| | Climax | Resolution |
|--|--------|-----------|
| **Function** | Confronts the conflict | Shows the aftermath |
| **Tone** | Maximum tension | Release, consequence |
| **Question** | Will the protagonist succeed? | What has changed? |

### Wilder's Perspective
- **Rule 10 (Tempo):** The climax must "build, build, build" in tempo and action until the last event. 
- **Rule 10 (The Exit):** Once the climax happens, "that's it." Don't hang around; the resolution must be brief.
- **Rule 3 (Active Goal):** The climax is the final proof of the protagonist's "clean line of action." They must drive the outcome, not be rescued.

## Examples
- **Alien (1979):** Ripley's final confrontation with the alien in the escape shuttle — active choice, real cost
- **Chinatown (1974):** The parking lot — everything the protagonist fought for is destroyed
- **Raiders of the Lost Ark (1981):** Indy chooses to close his eyes — victory through surrender
