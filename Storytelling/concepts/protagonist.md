# Protagonist

## Definition
The central character whose active choices and "clean line of action" drive the story from the inciting incident to the climax. The protagonist is not necessarily the "hero," but they are the primary engine of the plot.

## How It Works
A functional protagonist requires three specific structural components:

1. **The Clean Line of Action (Wilder Rule 3):** The character must have a clear, active goal. If they are merely reacting to events, the "line" is broken and the story sags.
2. **Polar Extremes (Frank Daniel):** An innovative arc is defined by movement between psychological poles (e.g., from *Selfishness* to *Sacrifice*).
3. **The Want vs. The Need:** - **Want:** The external goal (e.g., "Finding the Ark").
   - **Need:** The internal growth required to succeed (e.g., "Learning to believe in something bigger than oneself").

## Why It Matters
Without an active protagonist, the **[[concepts/three-act-structure|three-act structure]]** becomes a series of random occurrences. The audience only experiences **[[concepts/stakes|stakes]]** through the protagonist’s eyes; if the character doesn't care or isn't trying, the audience won't either.

## Synthesis: The Structural Bridge
Innovation in character design happens when we view the protagonist as a **Structural Function**:
- **Act 1:** The character's "flaw" creates the vulnerability that makes the **[[concepts/inciting-incident|inciting incident]]** possible.
- **Act 2:** The "Clean Line" provides the momentum.
- **Act 3:** The **[[concepts/climax|climax]]** is the final test—the character must choose their *Need* over their *Want* to resolve the conflict.

## Examples
- **Michael Corleone (The Godfather):** Moves between the poles of *Civilian Outsider* and *Criminal Head*. His choice to kill Sollozzo at the **[[concepts/midpoint|midpoint]]** is the definitive "Clean Line."
- **Ripley (Alien):** Moves from *Company Rule-Follower* to *Sole Survivor*. Her arc is driven by the professional stakes becoming physical survival.
