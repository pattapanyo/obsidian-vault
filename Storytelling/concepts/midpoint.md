# Midpoint

## Definition
A major structural event at the exact center of a screenplay (around page 55-60 of a 110-page script) that splits Act 2 into two halves and fundamentally shifts the story's direction or stakes.

## How It Works
The midpoint is not just a scene — it's a turning point that changes what the story is *about* on a deeper level. Two common forms:

| Form | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **False Peak** | Protagonist appears to succeed, but it creates new problems | Hero gets what they wanted but it's not enough |
| **False Collapse** | Situation suddenly worsens, raising the stakes dramatically | A key ally dies, a secret is revealed |

In [[concepts/eight-sequence-structure]] the midpoint falls between Sequence 4 and Sequence 5 — it is the hinge that transforms Act 2A (cautious hope) into Act 2B (escalating desperation).

## Why It Matters
Without a strong midpoint, Act 2 sags. The midpoint re-energizes the story and gives the audience a reason to stay engaged through the long middle. It also typically marks the moment the protagonist becomes *actively* committed rather than reactive.

## Examples
- **Raiders of the Lost Ark (1981):** Marion's apparent death at the midpoint shifts Indy from adventurer to man with nothing left to lose
- **Chinatown (1974):** Gittes discovers the water conspiracy goes all the way to the top — the personal case becomes political
- **The Godfather (1972):** Michael shoots Sollozzo — the midpoint that transforms him from civilian to criminal
