# Subtext

## Definition
What is communicated without being stated directly. The meaning beneath the surface of dialogue, action, and image. Subtext is the gap between what characters say and what they mean — and between what the story shows and what it implies.

## How It Works
Subtext operates on two levels:

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Character subtext** | A character says one thing but means another — driven by desire, fear, social constraint |
| **Structural subtext** | The story implies information the audience assembles themselves — the Lubitsch Touch |

The Lubitsch Touch — named after director Ernst Lubitsch and cited by [[entities/Billy Wilder]] as his most important lesson — is the practice of letting the audience complete the equation. Show two plus two, let them arrive at four themselves. The audience's act of completing the meaning creates stronger emotional investment than stating it outright.

## Why It Matters
Subtext is what separates sophisticated screenwriting from on-the-nose writing. When characters state their feelings directly, it deflates tension. When meaning is implied, the audience leans in. Trust in the audience's intelligence is the foundation of subtext.

Wilder's Rule 5 and Rule 7 are both about subtext:
- Rule 5: Hide your plot points elegantly
- Rule 7: Let the audience add up two plus two

## Examples
- **Some Like It Hot (1959):** The final line "Nobody's perfect" works entirely through subtext — the comedy and the pathos arrive simultaneously without either being stated
- **Chinatown (1974):** Evelyn Mulwray's eye twitch — subtext that signals something is wrong before it's revealed
- **The Godfather (1972):** Michael's transformation is shown through behavior, never announced
