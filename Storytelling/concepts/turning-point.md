# Turning Point

## Definition
A moment in the screenplay where the story shifts direction irreversibly. The protagonist's situation, goal, or understanding changes — what came before is no longer possible.

## How It Works
Turning points are the hinges of [[concepts/three-act-structure]]. Every act transition is driven by one:

| Turning Point | Location | Function |
|---------------|----------|----------|
| Act 1 → Act 2 | End of Sequence 2 | Locks protagonist into the story — no turning back |
| Midpoint | Center of Act 2 | Raises or collapses stakes, splits Act 2 in two |
| Act 2 → Act 3 | End of Sequence 6 | Darkest moment — forces the final choice |

A turning point is not just a plot event — it must change what the protagonist *wants or can do* going forward. If the story could continue the same way after the event, it's not a true turning point.

## Why It Matters
Turning points give the audience the sensation of forward momentum. Without them the story feels like a series of scenes rather than an escalating journey. They are also the primary diagnostic tool for structural problems — a sagging Act 2 almost always means a weak or misplaced turning point.

## Examples
- **Chinatown (1974):** Evelyn Mulwray's revelation about Katherine — turning point that recontextualizes everything
- **Alien (1979):** Kane's chest burster — the story is no longer about rescue, it's about survival
- **The Godfather (1972):** Michael volunteers to meet Sollozzo — turning point that commits him to the family business
