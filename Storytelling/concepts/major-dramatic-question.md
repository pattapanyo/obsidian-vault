# Major Dramatic Question (MDQ)

## Definition
The central "Yes/No" question raised by the **[[concepts/inciting-incident|inciting incident]]** that the audience spends the rest of the film waiting to see answered. It is the primary engine of audience engagement.

## How It Works
The MDQ provides the structural "spine" of the story:
1. **The Question (Act 1):** The inciting incident disrupts the world and asks the question (e.g., "Will the hero save the village?").
2. **The Delay (Act 2):** Complications and the **[[concepts/midpoint|midpoint]]** prevent an immediate answer, escalating the **[[concepts/stakes|stakes]]**.
3. **The Answer (Act 3):** The **[[concepts/climax|climax]]** provides the definitive "Yes" or "No."

## Why It Matters
Without a clear MDQ, Act 2 "sags" because the audience doesn't know what they are rooting for. The MDQ turns a series of events into a **[[concepts/protagonist|clean line of action]]** (Wilder Rule 3). If the question is answered too early, the story ends; if it is never asked, the audience disengages.

## Synthesis: Innovation via the "Double Payoff"
Innovation often occurs when the external MDQ and the internal **Need** (from the **[[concepts/protagonist|Protagonist]]** page) collide in Act 3:
- **The Bittersweet Ending:** The answer to the MDQ is "Yes," but the cost is the character’s internal Need (e.g., *The Godfather*: Michael saves the family, but loses his soul).
- **The Moral Victory:** The answer to the MDQ is "No," but the character achieves their internal growth (e.g., *Rocky*: He loses the fight, but proves he "wasn't just another bum from the neighborhood").

## Examples
- **Alien:** Will Ripley survive the alien? (Answer: Yes).
- **Chinatown:** Will Gittes solve the murder and save Evelyn? (Answer: No—the tragic subversion of the MDQ).
- **The Fugitive:** Will Dr. Richard Kimble find the one-armed man and clear his name? (Answer: Yes).
